Why this study matters

Behind every statistic is a Nigerian woman making a decision in the dark.

Around the world, roughly 73 million pregnancies end in abortion each year. Nearly half of those — 45% — are unsafe. In sub-Saharan Africa the figure rises to 77% (Bankole et al., 2020), and the region carries close to 70% of global maternal deaths.

Nigeria is the most populous country on the continent. Abortion here is illegal except to save the life of the pregnant woman, yet roughly 4.6% of women of reproductive age undergo a termination each year — close to 2 million abortions annually (Bell et al., 2020), most of them unsafe.

The Pamoja project — coordinated by RAES across nine African countries — works on four pillars: self-managed abortion support, advocacy, strategic litigation, and strategic communication. This poll exists to make that fourth pillar sharper, more honest, and more effective in Nigeria.

Most happen in silence — invisible to law, to data, and often to families. The cost is paid in lives, fertility, and futures.

A method designed for a sensitive conversation.

Finding 1 — Knowledge

People are getting the law wrong — in both directions.

In Nigeria, abortion is legally permitted only to save the life of the pregnant woman. But more than a third of respondents don't know that. And on every other circumstance — fetal anomaly, incest, rape, mental and physical health — most people wrongly believe abortion is already legal. This double error is dangerous in opposite ways.

What people think the law allows vs. reality

Percentage of respondents who incorrectly identify the legal status of abortion under each circumstance. Only the first item is actually legal in Nigeria.

Two errors, two consequences

The first error is fatal. 37% of respondents do not know that abortion is legal to save a woman's life — meaning women in life-threatening pregnancies may resign themselves to dying, or seek out unsafe back-channel abortions, when a legal option exists.

The second error is a brake on advocacy. When 49% of people believe abortion is already legal in cases of fetal anomaly, why would they push for reform? The false sense that the law already covers these cases creates what researchers call a "chilling effect" on organising — you can't fight for what you think you already have.

★ Why it matters

Communication can't only push for new rights. It also has to clarify what already exists — and where the gap between people's will and the law's reach is widest.

Finding 2 — Attitudes

The public is more open than the law lets on.

Despite intense stigma, large majorities support legalizing abortion in cases of fetal anomaly, incest, rape, and risk to a woman's life. Compared to earlier national polls, sentiment has shifted meaningfully toward openness — particularly in the southwest.

Support for legalizing abortion, by circumstance

Combined "Yes" + "Don't know" — i.e. respondents who do not oppose legalization. The non-opposition rate is the realistic ceiling for reform.

"Public support for legal termination of life-threatening pregnancies is extraordinarily high — 78% in favour. And yet women with dangerous pregnancies must navigate two independent doctors' opinions in less than twelve weeks. The disconnect between people's will and structural access is itself a site for action."

Finding 3 — The Persuadable Middle

One in three Nigerians is neither for nor against. They are the strategic core.

"Persuadables" (PMs) are respondents whose knowledge is partial and whose attitudes sit in neutral or near-neutral territory on the SABAS scales. They are not strangers to be converted — they are neighbours to be engaged.

Where the concept comes from

Mobilise the base. Persuade the persuadables. Don't waste energy on the opposition.

The "persuadable middle" is a foundational idea in advocacy and behaviour-change communication, popularised by organisers like George Lakey (the Spectrum of Allies) and Anat Shenker-Osorio ("fire up the base, persuade the persuadables, alienate the opposition"). The principle is simple: in any society, opinion on a contested issue is rarely binary. It sits on a spectrum — from active supporters at one end, to active opponents at the other, with several layers of soft support, neutrality, and soft opposition in between.

Trying to convert hardline opponents wastes resources. Preaching to the already-converted base feels good but doesn't move the needle. The actionable opportunity is the layer of passive allies, neutrals, and passive opponents — the persuadables — whose position is not yet locked in. Move enough of them, and the social norm shifts.

★ How Pamoja defined a "Persuadable" in this dataset

The Persuadable Middle isn't an abstract idea — it's a measurable segment. In this poll, a respondent was classified as a Persuadable if they met all three criteria below. Anyone outside these bounds was classified as a "Not Persuadable" (NP).

01
Partial knowledge
Got 4 or fewer correct on the 6 knowledge items about when abortion is legally permitted.
02
Not categorically opposed
14 or fewer "No" answers across the 16 legalization circumstances asked. Pure rejection on every front excludes someone from the persuadable group.
03
Neutral on stigma (SABAS)
A mean score between 2.5 and 3.5 on at least one of the two SABAS sub-scales (Negative Stereotyping or Exclusion & Discrimination). Neutrality, not approval — they have not yet hardened.

Applying these three criteria as a binary filter identified 384 Persuadables (32%) and 816 Not Persuadables (68%) in the sample of 1,200.

Why this audience matters more than any other

The committed opposition (the "Not Persuadables" — 68% of the sample) won't move easily, and the non-religious tertiary-educated minority is too small and too unrepresentative to drive broad social norm change.

The Persuadable Middle is large enough to matter (n = 384), demographically mainstream, and — critically — moderately religious. That last point reframes the entire communication challenge: the work isn't to argue people out of their faith. It's to show them models of faith that include compassion for women.

★ Strategic implication

Stop framing this as "religion vs. rights." Start telling journey stories from people of faith who came to support safe abortion — modelling identification, not opposition. The more such role models exist, the more social norms shift.

Persuadable Middle by state — where to invest first

State very highly significantly predicts whether a respondent is persuadable. Oyo and Osun lead — these are the states where each communication dollar reaches the most movable opinion. Ondo, by contrast, is the hardest ground.

Finding 4 — Media habits

Where audiences actually are — and what they're hungry for.

82% of Southwest Nigerians watch TV or streaming content. Most of that consumption now flows through social platforms. But radio still matters — not for everyone, but for very specific audiences.

How TV/streaming content is accessed

Among the 82% who watch TV or streaming. Multiple answers possible.

Most-watched content formats

Short-form video dominates. Reality shows underperform.

Audiences want African content, especially comedy.

72% of respondents agree that creators should make more shows set in Africa, vs. only 55% for shows set elsewhere. When asked open-endedly about their favourite thing to watch, 25% spontaneously named African content — Yoruba films, Nollywood, named comedians.

Reality TV is consistently the least-watched format. The ground for behaviour change content isn't drama-fatigue audiences; it's people scrolling short videos and seeking laughter, recognition, and stories that look like their lives.

Radio still matters — for who, and for what.

59% of respondents listen to radio, dwarfed by TV/streaming (82%). But radio listeners skew older, more religious, and secondary-educated — the same profile linked to higher stigma. This is not a coincidence. Radio is where reinforcement of conservative norms happens most.

Most popular radio programming: News (63%), Talk shows (40%), Morning shows (35%), then music, sports, late-night, and dramas. Top stations: Fresh FM, Wazobia FM, Adaba FM, Unique FM.

Pamoja's outreach is registering — but reaching only 8% of residents.

Residents of Pamoja states (Lagos, Ogun) were significantly more likely to recall abortion-rights campaigns than non-residents. Compared to CARE's recent campaign in Yobe (which celebrated 8% engagement as a win), this is a real signal — but the runway for growth is wide.

What to do with this

Six strategic directions for partners.

These are not generic best practices. Each one is anchored in a specific finding from this dataset and a specific gap in current Pamoja activity.

DIRECTION 01

Lead with what the law actually says.

Before pushing reform, fix the basics. Most people don't know that abortion is legal even to save a woman's life. Visualize the law in plain language across short-form video, posters, radio drama, and clinic waiting-room content.

DIRECTION 02

Mobilise the "low-hanging" reform consensus.

Majorities don't oppose legalization for fetal anomaly (68%), incest (63%), and rape (58%). This is the ground where advocacy and litigation can build the most public support fastest, aligning with the Maputo Protocol.

DIRECTION 03

Build content for short-form video first.

57% of respondents watch short-form video. Distribute across Facebook (58%), TikTok (55%), and YouTube (52%). It's the most popular, the most accessible, and the cheapest to produce. Hour-long Nollywood-style drama remains a strong second format.

DIRECTION 04

Tell journey stories from moderately religious voices.

The Persuadable Middle is 69% moderately religious. Don't argue dogma — model evolution. First-person testimonials from imams, pastors, Catholic women, and lay believers who came to support compassion for women are the highest-leverage content type.

DIRECTION 05

Lead investment with Oyo and Osun.

These two states have the highest concentration of Persuadables (41% and 40%). Without abandoning Lagos and Ogun, expand the visible field of Pamoja activity into these states where each engagement reaches more movable minds.

DIRECTION 06

Engage women on internalised stigma — gently.

Women showed significantly higher exclusion-and-discrimination scores than men. Reaching women's networks (faith groups, market associations, women's cooperatives) with stories of solidarity rather than confrontation is essential to shifting the social norms women themselves enforce.

"Rather than push out sad stories intended to foster empathy for women who seek to end pregnancies, Pamoja should push out morality-inflected stories intended to foster admiration for the people of faith who modelled bravery and an unshakable commitment to compassion."

— Discussion section, RAES / Pamoja Opinion Poll Report 2025
